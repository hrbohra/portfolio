<Step n={1} phase="constraint">
  <Plain>
    The furniture client's new catalogue had 7,975 product photos from a dozen suppliers,
    and none of them knew what they were pictures of. Sofa or bed, dining or fabric
    swatch: a human sorting them at a steady pace needs weeks. The website launch needed
    them sorted now, and the budget for cloud AI services was zero.
  </Plain>
  <Eng>
    Classification task: 7,975 images into 7 categories (bedroom, living, dining, chairs,
    fabrics and swatches, mattresses, occasional). Constraints: zero cloud spend, one
    consumer machine, a weekend of wall-clock time, and no tolerance for a half-finished
    run. A vision LLM run locally fits all four if, and only if, the pipeline around it
    is solid.
  </Eng>
</Step>

<Step n={2} phase="design">
  <Plain>
    The AI model is the glamorous part and the easy part: a free, capable vision model
    running on my own machine, looking at each photo and naming what it sees. The real
    work is wrapping it in something that survives a multi-hour run: several images
    processed at once, and a running record of what is finished, so a crash costs
    minutes, not the weekend.
  </Plain>
  <Eng>
    Local Ollama serving qwen2.5vl:7b, parallel workers feeding it, and a checkpoint file
    as the source of truth: every completed image is recorded in progress.json, and
    startup resumes from the checkpoint rather than from zero. The batch is designed
    around the assumption that a multi-hour job on a consumer machine will be
    interrupted. Same philosophy as my ETL work: assume interruption, make restarts
    cheap.
  </Eng>
</Step>

<Step n={3} phase="incident">
  <Plain>
    The run did get interrupted, more than once, over its multi-hour life. Each time, it
    picked up where it stopped, exactly as designed. There is a special calm in watching
    a system fail the way you planned for.
  </Plain>
  <Eng>
    Multiple interruptions across the multi-hour batch, all absorbed by checkpoint
    resume. No duplicate work, no lost work, no manual bookkeeping. The throughput chart
    in the evidence panel shows the restarts as flat spots, not resets.
  </Eng>
</Step>

<Step n={4} phase="verified">
  <Plain>
    By the end of the weekend: all 7,975 images sorted into the 7 categories, none left
    over, total AI bill of zero pounds. The categories went straight into the website's
    catalogue build, where they still do their job today.
  </Plain>
  <Eng>
    7,975/7,975 classified, 7 categories, 0 uncategorised, £0 inference cost. Output CSV
    feeds the storefront's offline catalogue builder. The honest caveat, stated because
    honesty scales: a 7B local model is not perfect, and the taxonomy was designed so
    that boundary mistakes between adjacent categories are cheap to correct downstream.
  </Eng>
</Step>

<Takeaway>
  Pragmatic ML. The model was the easy half; the checkpointed pipeline was the work.
  Knowing when a small local model plus solid engineering beats an expensive API is
  itself an engineering skill.
</Takeaway>
