<Step n={1} phase="symptom">
  <Plain>
    I was the founding engineer on a crypto wallet, three weeks from launch. One morning,
    every token swap stopped working. Not one coin. Not one network. Every swap, on all
    seven networks the wallet supported, failed. Users would have seen their money simply
    refuse to move.
  </Plain>
  <Eng>
    Gasless swap submissions began failing across all 7 EVM networks simultaneously.
    Orders were built, signed and submitted, then rejected at fill. Nothing in our code
    had shipped that morning. A failure this uniform pointed at something shared by every
    chain path, which is a short list: the SDK, the signing flow, or the provider.
  </Eng>
</Step>

<Step n={2} phase="dead-end">
  <Plain>
    The obvious suspects came first. Was our connection to each blockchain misconfigured?
    Hours went into checking that. Nothing. Was the network fee estimation wrong, so
    transactions died before running? More hours. Also nothing. Both reasonable guesses.
    Both wrong. I keep my dead ends in the write-up because this is what real debugging
    looks like.
  </Plain>
  <Eng>
    Hypothesis 1: per-chain RPC misconfiguration. Ruled out, endpoints healthy, reads and
    unrelated writes fine on every chain. Hypothesis 2: gas estimation. Ruled out, these
    are gasless orders filled by a relayer, and fills were being rejected, not reverting
    on gas. The uniformity across chains kept saying the same thing: stop looking at
    chain-specific config.
  </Eng>
</Step>

<Step n={3} phase="root-cause">
  <Plain>
    The real cause sat in the fine print of a partner system. The service that fills swaps
    had changed how it derives the address that signs an order. Our wallet signed orders
    one way. The filler now reconstructed the signer a different way. The two no longer
    matched, so every order looked forged and was rejected. On every chain, because every
    chain used the same signing flow.
  </Plain>
  <Eng>
    The liquidity provider's flow had moved to ProxyFactory-derived maker addresses.
    Signature recovery on the filled order no longer resolved to the EOA that signed the
    EIP-712 payload, so every order failed maker verification. I traced it through the SDK
    source and signature-recovery flows rather than our application code. The bug was not
    in our repo, which is exactly why no diff explained it.
  </Eng>
</Step>

<Step n={4} phase="fix">
  <Plain>
    Rewriting our whole signing system under deadline would have been the panic move. The
    calm move was smaller: convert the coin at the very start of the swap so the rest of
    the journey stayed on the standard, well-tested path. One extra step for the wallet,
    zero exotic code, and the partner system was happy again. I also rebuilt the order
    flow so nothing stale could linger from before the fix.
  </Plain>
  <Eng>
    For native-ETH swaps I added a WETH pre-wrap, keeping the standard ERC-20 signing path
    end to end and avoiding the ProxyFactory maker mismatch entirely. Alongside it, a
    stateless rebuild of order construction eliminated a stale order cache that the outage
    had exposed. Scope stayed deliberately narrow: preserve the audited path, do not fork
    the signing flow three weeks before launch.
  </Eng>
</Step>

<Step n={5} phase="verified">
  <Plain>
    Swaps came back the same day. Later, with real money, I ran the full journey on a live
    network myself: approve, quote, sign, submit, filled. Watching actual funds convert
    correctly is the only test that fully counts in this business.
  </Plain>
  <Eng>
    Execution restored same day across all 7 networks. Verification was the full mainnet
    flow with live funds on Base: allowance check, approval tx, quote, EIP-712 sign,
    relayer submit, filled. USDC to WETH. The incident and the fix went into a written
    post-mortem with a how-to-revert section, like every change on this project.
  </Eng>
</Step>

<Takeaway>
  Systematic isolation under deadline pressure. When everything fails at once, the cause
  lives in what everything shares. And the right fix is often the smallest one that keeps
  you on the well-tested path.
</Takeaway>
