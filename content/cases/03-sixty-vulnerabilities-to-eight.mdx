<Step n={1} phase="constraint">
  <Plain>
    A crypto wallet is a promise: your money and your identity are safe here. Before
    launch, I treated that promise as my job even though no ticket said so. I went looking
    for the ways a stranger could hurt our users, in our own code and in everything we had
    borrowed.
  </Plain>
  <Eng>
    Pre-launch security pass, self-initiated. Scope: the API surface (authorisation and
    session handling), and the dependency tree. Constraint: launch timeline unchanged, so
    findings needed triage discipline, not a rewrite.
  </Eng>
</Step>

<Step n={2} phase="finding">
  <Plain>
    Two kinds of problem turned up. First, doors that opened for the wrong people: places
    where asking for someone else's data politely enough would hand it over. Second, old
    locks: dozens of known weaknesses in third-party building blocks we depended on, sixty
    of them when counted honestly.
  </Plain>
  <Eng>
    Finding one: IDOR on user-scoped endpoints. Authenticated requests could address other
    users' resources by identifier; authorisation checks were missing at the object level.
    Finding two: 60 known vulnerabilities across dependencies. Finding three: session
    tokens lived too long and could not be revoked, which turns any single leak into a
    long-term compromise.
  </Eng>
</Step>

<Step n={3} phase="fix">
  <Plain>
    The doors got locks that check who you are on every request, not just at the entrance.
    Sessions became short-lived and revocable, so a stolen key expires before it matters.
    The sixty old locks got sorted into three honest piles: fix now, replace the part, or
    accept knowingly with a written reason. Pretending you will fix all sixty is how none
    get fixed.
  </Plain>
  <Eng>
    IDOR closed with object-level authorisation on every user-scoped route. Session auth
    migrated to short-lived JWTs with refresh-token rotation and revocation. Dependencies
    triaged explicitly: patched where updates existed, replaced where maintainers had
    gone quiet, accepted with written rationale where exploitability did not apply to our
    usage. 60 became 8, and the 8 have documentation, not silence.
  </Eng>
</Step>

<Step n={4} phase="verified">
  <Plain>
    Every fix was tested by trying the attack again and watching it fail. The whole pass
    was written up, including a threat model: a document that asks, in advance, how bad
    each kind of break-in would be and how hard it is to pull off.
  </Plain>
  <Eng>
    Each IDOR fix verified by replaying the original access pattern and confirming denial.
    The pass produced a threat-model document covering, among other things, OTP
    brute-force probability and hashing hardness. Residual risk is enumerated, not
    implied. Security as habit, not checkbox.
  </Eng>
</Step>

<Takeaway>
  Security work is triage plus honesty. Sixty to eight matters less than the fact that
  the remaining eight are named, understood and accepted on paper.
</Takeaway>
