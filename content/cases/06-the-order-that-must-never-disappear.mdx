<Step n={1} phase="question">
  <Plain>
    This case is not a bug hunt. It is a design decision, which is the other half of
    engineering. The shop takes real payments. The order used to travel to the shop's
    staff by email. So I asked the paranoid question on purpose: a customer pays, and at
    that exact moment our email provider is having a bad day. Where is the order? The
    honest answer was: nowhere. Money taken, order gone, and nobody would even know.
  </Plain>
  <Eng>
    Failure-mode analysis on the post-payment path. Stripe confirms the charge via
    webhook; fulfilment notification went out by email. Email was the only durable record
    on our side. An email-provider outage in that window meant a paid order with no
    persisted trace and no operator signal. Probability low, cost unacceptable,
    detectability zero. That combination is what you design against.
  </Eng>
</Step>

<Step n={2} phase="design">
  <Plain>
    The fix is an ordering rule: write it down first, tell people second. The moment a
    payment lands, the order is saved somewhere durable before any email is attempted.
    Then staff get their notification. If saving fails, or the email fails, an alarm goes
    to me either way. And staff no longer depend on their inbox: there is an admin page
    that lists every order straight from storage.
  </Plain>
  <Eng>
    Persist-before-notify. The signature-verified webhook writes the order to durable KV
    before any email attempt. An /admin/orders viewer reads from KV directly, removing
    the inbox as a single point of failure. Sentry alerts fire on persistence failure and
    on email failure independently: both legs are observable. Admin access sits behind
    HMAC-cookie auth with a written security assessment.
  </Eng>
</Step>

<Step n={3} phase="guarantee">
  <Plain>
    The result is a promise you can state in one sentence: if the customer paid, the
    order exists, and if anything went wrong around it, a human finds out. Weaker systems
    promise "it usually works". Guarantees are what let a two-person shop sleep.
  </Plain>
  <Eng>
    Invariant: charge confirmed implies order persisted, or an alert has fired. No path
    exists where payment succeeds and the order vanishes silently. The design writes the
    invariant first and derives the sequence from it, not the other way round.
  </Eng>
</Step>

<Step n={4} phase="verified">
  <Plain>
    Tested by rehearsing the disaster: simulate the email service failing on a paid
    order, watch the order survive in storage and the alarm arrive. Verification means
    making the bad day happen on purpose, in rehearsal.
  </Plain>
  <Eng>
    Verified by fault injection: email path failed deliberately on a test order,
    order present in KV, Sentry alert received. The storefront's checkout also validates
    prices server-side and enforces minimum quantities authoritatively in the API, part
    of the same principle: the server owns the truth.
  </Eng>
</Step>

<Takeaway>
  Thinking in guarantees, not happy paths. The valuable question was not "does it work"
  but "what survives when the third party under it fails".
</Takeaway>

<ProductClose>
  Ask your team what happens to a paid order if your email provider dies for an hour.
  If the answer takes longer than a sentence, that is the engineer you are missing.
</ProductClose>
