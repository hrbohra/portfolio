## What it is

<Plain>
Technicon Furnishers is a real furniture company with two shops in the South Wales valleys.
They needed to sell online. A five-person team elsewhere had spent a year on it without
shipping. I took it over and built it solo: a full store carrying roughly 6,434 products from
12 different suppliers, live at cwtchcomfort.com.
</Plain>
<Eng>
Next.js 14 App Router storefront for a VAT-registered two-branch retailer. The catalogue is
built offline by a Python pipeline that normalises 12 heterogeneous supplier sources into one
schema; the site consumes the built catalogue with no database. Deployed on Vercel with
Sentry across client, server and edge.
</Eng>

## What was built

<Plain>
Everything a furniture shop needs online: browsing that handles thousands of products without
feeling like a warehouse, product pages led by photography, and configurators that let a
customer build their own sofa, bed or wardrobe and watch the price update as they choose.
Then the unglamorous parts that make it a business: card payments, delivery rules by
postcode, orders that cannot be lost, and the legal pages.
</Plain>
<Eng>
Mega-menu navigation, per-category landing pages, faceted /shop, photography-led PDPs with a
finish switcher and tiered range rail. A two-axis sofa configurator (configuration times
colour), a Weimann bed builder, and a wardrobe configurator with live pricing including VAT.
Stripe-hosted checkout with server-side price validation (rejects enquire-only and zero-price
items, enforces per-product minimum quantities authoritatively in the API) and
signature-verified webhooks. Postcode-gated delivery via a KV allowlist, collection option,
enquiry flows for made-to-order items. Orders persist to KV before any email attempt, with an
admin viewer and Sentry alerts on both paths. HMAC-cookie admin auth with a written security
assessment. Consent-gated GA4 with purchase conversion events, CSP and security-header
middleware, JSON-LD, sitemap and canonical SEO, and a styled error boundary. I also authored
the 19-clause online conditions of sale and the returns, delivery and contact pages.
</Eng>

## The hard parts

<Plain>
Two are written up as cases: the day I caught my own site shipping the entire catalogue to
every visitor's phone, and the design decision that means a paid order can never silently
vanish, even if our email provider dies at the worst moment.
</Plain>
<Eng>
See the linked cases. The general pattern of this project: a solo engineer is also the
reviewer, so I run scheduled audits of my own production work and treat the findings exactly
as if a colleague had shipped them.
</Eng>
